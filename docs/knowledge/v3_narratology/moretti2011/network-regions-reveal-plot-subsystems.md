# Network regions reveal plot subsystems

Source: Moretti 2011, "Network Theory, Plot Analysis"

When you build a character network, clusters of interconnected characters form visible **regions** — subsystems within the plot that share some structural property. These regions often correspond to meaningful narrative units (political factions, families, social circles) that aren't explicitly labeled in the text.

In *Hamlet*, the "region of death" emerges: characters connected to both Claudius and Hamlet (Polonius, Gertrude, Laertes, Ophelia, Rosencrantz, Guildenstern) are all killed. The network makes this visible as a structural property — "what is truly deadly is the characters' position in the network, chained to the warring poles of king and prince."

The Court hexagon (Hamlet, Claudius, Gertrude, Polonius, Ophelia, Laertes) forms the densest region with 100% clustering. Outside this dense core, peripheral characters like Horatio inhabit sparser regions with different structural properties.

## Key insight

Regions are emergent — they arise from the pattern of connections, not from authorial labels. ==This means network analysis can discover groupings the author may not have consciously designed.==
