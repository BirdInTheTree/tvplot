# Symmetry emerges naturally in small chapter-level networks

Source: Moretti 2011, "Network Theory, Plot Analysis"

When Moretti switches from whole-play networks to chapter-level networks in Dickens (*Our Mutual Friend*), a striking pattern appears: binary symmetry. Chapters consistently organize around pairs — husband and wife, parent and child, brother and sister, suitor and beloved, rival and rival.

Two reasons for this:
1. **Dickens builds from binary pairs** — relationships are dualistic by nature
2. **With few characters, there's little "noise" to disrupt the pattern** — the binaries project cleanly onto the chapter structure

This symmetry in Dickens contrasts with the **asymmetry** Moretti finds in *The Story of the Stone* (Chinese novel). Chinese aesthetic tradition values symmetry explicitly — "parallel prose," hemispheric structure — but with hundreds of characters per chapter, the symmetry becomes local (within scenes) while the chapter as a whole is asymmetric.

## The principle

Symmetry and character count are inversely related: symmetry emerges with few characters, breaks down with many. "If with few characters symmetry emerges almost by itself, with *many* characters it becomes implausible."
