# Weak ties connect narrative regions and point beyond the main story world

Source: Moretti 2011, "Network Theory, Plot Analysis"

Horatio in *Hamlet* is the paradigmatic weak tie: low clustering, few intense connections, but a wide radius. His connections are "less intense, but with a wider radius; and more impersonal, almost bureaucratic" — like the ties Graham Sack described in *Bleak House*.

The peripheral characters connected through weak ties often point to the world **beyond** the central story world. In *Hamlet*, the characters with the fewest connections (one link to the network) are links to places beyond Elsinore: Cornelius and Voltemand to Norway, Reynaldo to France, the sailor to England. These "centrifugal threads" are the "tendrils" that gesture toward the larger world.

Horatio's function is unique: "a function in the plot, but not a motivation. No aim, no emotions — no *language*, really, worthy of *Hamlet*." He's structurally critical but narratively flat — his style matches the State bureaucracy, not the Court's melodrama.

## Key insight

Weak ties serve a dual function in narrative:
1. **Structural**: they hold the network together by bridging disconnected regions
2. **Thematic**: they represent the outside world, the context beyond the main conflict
