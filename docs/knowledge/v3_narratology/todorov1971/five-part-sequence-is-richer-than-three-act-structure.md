# The five-part sequence is richer than the three-act structure

Source: Todorov 1971, "The 2 Principles of Narrative" (pp. 38-39), also Todorov's analysis of the Decameron

The simplified "Todorov model" (equilibrium → disruption → new equilibrium) is widely taught but misses two crucial middle steps. The full five-part sequence:

1. **Initial equilibrium** — a stable situation
2. **Disruption** — a force disturbs the equilibrium
3. **Recognition** — the disruption is acknowledged, understood
4. **Attempt to repair** — action taken to restore or create new order
5. **New equilibrium** — a different stable situation (not the same as the initial one)

Steps 3 and 4 are what most summaries drop, but they're structurally important. The *recognition* that something has gone wrong is a separate narrative moment from the disruption itself. And the *attempt to repair* is where most of the narrative action happens — it's where characters exercise agency.

## Comparison with other models

| Todorov (5 parts) | Freytag (7 functions) | Bremond (3 phases) |
|---|---|---|
| Initial equilibrium | setup | — |
| Disruption | inciting_incident | possibility |
| Recognition | — (no equivalent) | — |
| Attempt to repair | escalation, turning_point, crisis | realization |
| New equilibrium | resolution | result |

The key difference: Todorov adds **recognition** — the moment when characters (and the audience) realize that the equilibrium has been disturbed. This is narratively distinct from the disruption itself. In Breaking Bad, Walt getting cancer is the disruption; Walt *deciding to cook meth* is the recognition + beginning of repair attempt.

Our pipeline's "inciting_incident" conflates disruption and recognition. Todorov would split them.

## What this means for tvplotlines

In practice, this means the current "inciting_incident" function may hide two distinct structural moments. For some plotlines, separating "what went wrong" (disruption) from "when the character decided to act" (recognition) could clarify the plotline's structure. This connects to Bal/Greimas's concept of **competence**: recognition is when the subject acquires *determination* (the will to act).
