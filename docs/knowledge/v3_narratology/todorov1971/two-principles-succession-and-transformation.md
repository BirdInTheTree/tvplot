# The two principles of narrative are succession and transformation

Source: Todorov 1971, "The 2 Principles of Narrative", Diacritics 1(1), pp. 37-44

Todorov argues that narrative rests on two irreducible principles:

**Succession** — events follow each other in time. One thing after another. This is the temporal axis: the "and then... and then..." of narrative.

**Transformation** — one state changes into another. A proposition is modified. This is the causal/logical axis: something *becomes* something else.

Pure succession without transformation is a chronicle — one event after another, but nothing fundamentally changes. Pure transformation without succession would be simultaneous changes — more like a tableau than a story.

Narrative is the interplay of both: events succeed each other in time *and* each event transforms the situation. The interest of narrative comes from transformation; its intelligibility comes from succession.

## Why this is more precise than "beginning-middle-end"

The usual summary of Todorov — "equilibrium → disruption → new equilibrium" — is a simplification. His actual claim is that the fundamental narrative unit is **transformation**: the change from proposition X to proposition X'. The five-part sequence (equilibrium → disruption → recognition → repair attempt → new equilibrium) is one *pattern* of transformation, not the definition.

## What this means for tvplotlines

Our seven functions (setup → resolution) capture succession well — events are ordered along a timeline. But they don't explicitly capture transformation. What state changed? From what to what?

Adding a **transformation** field to events — "X was A, now X is B" — would capture what Todorov considers the core of narrative. Examples:
- "Walt strangles Krazy-8" → transformation: Walt [innocent → killer]
- "Skyler organizes an intervention" → transformation: Family [ignoring cancer → confronting cancer]

This is the same insight as Bal's "event = transition between states," but Todorov makes the *from-state* and *to-state* explicit.
