# Gnoseological narrative is driven by knowledge, not action

Source: Todorov 1971, "The 2 Principles of Narrative" (pp. 39-42), from analysis of The Quest for the Grail

Todorov identifies a distinct type of narrative: the **gnoseological** (knowledge-driven) narrative, where the organizing principle is not "what happens" but "what is learned."

In The Quest for the Grail, the principal narrative is "a narrative of knowledge, not of action." Characters seek to understand the meaning of what they encounter. The events of the quest "are merely occasions for the exercise of interpretive faculties." The real narrative movement is from ignorance to understanding — not from state A to state B through physical action.

Key characteristics of gnoseological narrative:
- Events matter not for their consequences but for their **meaning**
- The central transformation is cognitive: not-knowing → knowing
- "It is not possible to bear one's self upon the search for concealment. But one can organize it around the requirement that he cause as little pain as possible" — the moral dimension is about knowledge management, not action
- The MRE (most reportable event) is a **recognition**, not an action

## Three types of actions in the Grail

Todorov distinguishes three types:
1. **Supernatural/given** — the Grail itself, divine intervention (not caused by characters)
2. **Human agency** — characters' deliberate actions
3. **Ritual/prescribed** — actions dictated by social/religious code

In the gnoseological narrative, type 1 provides the *object* of knowledge, type 2 the *attempt* to understand, and type 3 the *framework* for interpretation.

## What this means for tvplotlines

This is directly relevant to **detective/procedural shows** (House, CSI, True Detective) and **mystery arcs** in serial TV (Lost, Twin Peaks, Westworld). In these narratives, the plotline's "events" are primarily moments of recognition, not moments of action:

- "House orders a test" is an action, but the narrative transformation is: House [doesn't know diagnosis → has new hypothesis]
- "Hank finds the desert cooking site" is an action, but narratively it's: Hank [ignorant of Walt → closer to discovering Walt]

Our current event definition — "one action by one character that changes the situation" — captures the action but not the knowledge transformation. For procedural/mystery plotlines, the *knowledge state* is more important than the physical state.

This could inform how we classify events in procedural shows: alongside the physical action, track the **knowledge transformation** — who learns what, and how does that change the narrative landscape.

## Relevance to suspense types

This connects directly to Bal's four suspense types: gnoseological narrative is built on the **riddle** (reader−, character−) and **secret** (reader−, character+) types. The narrative tension comes from knowledge asymmetry, not from physical threat.
