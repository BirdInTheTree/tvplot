# Postclassical narratology fragmented into many strands after 1999

Source: Ionescu 2019, "Postclassical Narratology: Twenty Years Later", Word and Text IX, pp. 5-34

Since Herman coined "postclassical narratology" in 1997, the field has splintered into numerous subfields, each with its own methods and priorities:

- **Rhetorical narratology** (Booth, Phelan, Rabinowitz) — focuses on the implied author, authorial audience, ethical dimensions of narration. "The flesh and blood reader seeks to enter the authorial audience in order to understand the invitations for engagement that the narrative offers" (p. 10)
- **Cognitive narratology** (Fludernik, Herman) — uses mental processes and cognitive science to understand how readers construct storyworlds
- **Corpus narratology** (Toolan, Sinclair, Stubbs) — stylistic analysis of large corpora, computational methods
- **Feminist narratology** (Lanser, Page) — gender differences in story/discourse
- **Transmedial narratology** (Helbig, Ryan, Rajewsky) — narrative across media: literature, film, comics, videogames. "Seek to rebuild narratology so that it can handle new genres and storytelling practices across a wide spectrum of media" (p. 14)
- **Digital narratology** — hypertext narratives, interactive fiction, videogames
- **Unnatural narratology** (Richardson, Alber) — anti-mimetic narratives that challenge real-world understandings of identity, time, space
- **Corporeal narratology** (Punday) — the role of the body in narrative

## The classical/postclassical distinction

Classical narratology (Propp, Barthes, Genette, Todorov, Greimas, Bal) sought **universal narrative structures**. Postclassical narratology doesn't reject these but "rethinks their conceptual underpinnings and reassesses their scope of applicability" (Herman 1999). Key additions: context-sensitivity, cognitive dimensions, cross-media applicability, ethical considerations.

Herman's definition: postclassical narratology "contains classical narratology as one of its 'moments' but is marked by a profusion of new methodologies and research hypotheses" (p. 9).

## What this means for tvplotlines

We are doing something that sits at the intersection of several strands:
- **Classical structuralist** — using Bal, Todorov, Bremond, Greimas for formal models
- **Cognitive** — our pipeline implicitly constructs storyworlds from synopses (Herman's experientiality)
- **Transmedial** — applying literary concepts to TV series
- **Corpus/computational** — automated analysis at scale

The survey confirms that no single narratological framework is authoritative. Our approach of layering multiple classical frameworks (Bremond's cycles + Bal's actants + Labov's evaluation + Todorov's transformation) while keeping them operationally simple for LLM prompts is a defensible position — it's what postclassical narratology recommends: use classical tools but don't treat them as universal laws.

The strand we're *not* drawing on but could: **rhetorical narratology** (Phelan) — how the narrative invites specific judgments and emotional responses from the audience. This connects to Herman's "what it's like" and Labov's "evaluation." It's about the *intended effect* of narrative choices on the viewer. For TV analysis, this is showrunner intent — why this scene exists, what reaction it's designed to produce.
