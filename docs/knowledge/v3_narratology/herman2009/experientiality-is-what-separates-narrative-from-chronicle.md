# Experientiality is what separates narrative from chronicle

Source: Herman 2009, "Basic Elements of Narrative", Ch. 1 (pp. 21-22)

Herman's fourth element — **"what it's like"** — is the most cognitively oriented and arguably the most distinctive claim of the book. A narrative doesn't just tell what happened; it conveys **what it was like to experience** the events from within the storyworld.

"The less markedly a text or a discourse encodes the pressure of events on an experiencing human or at least human-like consciousness, the less amenable that text or discourse will be to interpretation in narrative terms" (p. 21).

Without experientiality:
- A temporal sequence of events is a **chronicle** or **report**
- A description of general patterns is an **explanation**
- A series of states without an experiencing consciousness is a **list**

With experientiality: the same events become a **story** — because someone *lived through* them and was affected by them.

## Connection to Fludernik

Herman builds on Fludernik's (1996) concept of "experientiality" as the core of narrativity. For Fludernik, "there can be narratives without plot, but there cannot be any narratives without a human (or anthropomorphic) experiencer of some sort" (quoted on p. xvi). This is a strong claim: experientiality is more fundamental to narrative than event structure.

## What this means for tvplotlines

This diagnoses our biggest theoretical gap. Our pipeline extracts *what happens* (events, functions, plotlines) but not *what it's like* to experience those events. Synopses inherently strip experientiality: "Walt strangles Krazy-8" is an event report, not a narrative. The narrative is what it's like for Walt to do this — the horror, the necessity, the threshold crossed.

Practically, we can't fully recover experientiality from synopses. But we could:
1. Ask the LLM to infer the **experiential impact** of events on characters (fear, relief, shock, shame)
2. Track **whose consciousness** each event primarily impacts — this connects to Bal's focalization
3. Use experiential intensity as a signal for identifying the MRE — the most intense experiential moment is likely the most reportable event

This also explains why our "theme" field in pass2 often feels thin: theme emerges from the experiential dimension (what it was like), which we don't capture. The theme of Breaking Bad S01E03 isn't just "the illusion of control" as an abstract concept — it's the specific experience of discovering that control is an illusion through the act of killing someone.
