# Breach of a canonical script makes events reportable

Source: Herman 2009, "Basic Elements of Narrative", Ch. 1 (pp. 19-21), drawing on Bruner 1990/1991

Herman adopts Bruner's concept of **"canonicity and breach"**: narratives are worth telling because an implicit canonical script — what was expected, what normally happens — has been violated. The function of the story is then "to find an intentional state that mitigates or at least makes comprehensible a deviation from a canonical cultural pattern" (Bruner 1990: 49-50).

This is more precise than Todorov's "disruption of equilibrium." It's not just that something changes — it's that something changes **against expectations**. The canonical script is what characters (and the audience) expect to happen based on the storyworld's norms. The breach is what actually happens instead.

Bruner: "when you encounter an exception to the ordinary, and ask somebody what is happening, the person you ask will virtually always tell a story that contains *reasons* (or some other specification of an intentional state)."

## Post hoc ergo propter hoc

Herman, following Barthes (p. 8), notes that "the mainspring of narrative is precisely the confusion of consecution and consequence, what comes *after* being read in narrative as what is *caused by*." Readers systematically treat temporal sequence as causal connection. Narrative exploits this: if event B follows event A, readers assume A caused B unless told otherwise.

## What this means for tvplotlines

This gives us a sharper test for "inciting_incident" and for what makes an event narratively significant:

**Current test**: "the event that starts the plotline"
**With canonicity and breach**: "the event that violates the canonical expectation of the storyworld"

In Breaking Bad: the canonical script is "high school teacher lives a quiet life." The cancer diagnosis *breaches* this script. But the truly narrative breach is Walt *deciding to cook meth* — that's where the canonical expectation of how a teacher responds to illness is shattered.

This also explains why some events feel more "dramatic" than others: they violate stronger canonical expectations. A murder in a crime show has low breach (canonical for the genre). A murder in a sitcom has high breach (violates genre norms). The *strength of breach* could help with plotline ranking — the A-story has the strongest canonical breach.
