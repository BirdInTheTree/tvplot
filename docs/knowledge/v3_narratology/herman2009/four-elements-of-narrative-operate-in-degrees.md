# Four elements of narrative operate in degrees, not as binary features

Source: Herman 2009, "Basic Elements of Narrative", Ch. 1 (pp. 9-22)

Herman proposes four basic elements that constitute conditions for narrativity. Crucially, they are **gradient** — a text is more or less prototypically narrative based on how strongly each element is present:

**(i) Situatedness** — the narrative is situated in a specific discourse context or occasion for telling. The same story told in court testimony vs. a screenplay vs. a party anecdote is interpreted differently because the communicative situation shapes meaning.

**(ii) Event sequencing** — a structured time-course of particularized events. Not just "things happen" but particular things happen to particular people at particular times. Narrative traces "paths taken by particularized individuals faced with decision points at one or more temporal junctures" (p. 19). The temporal profile distinguishes narrative from description (which can be reordered freely) and explanation (which concerns general patterns, not particular cases).

**(iii) Worldmaking / world disruption** — events introduce disruption or disequilibrium into a storyworld. Building on Propp (disruptive events as motor of narrative) and Todorov (equilibrium → disruption → new equilibrium), Herman adds Bruner's concept of **"canonicity and breach"**: a story is worth telling because "an implicit canonical script has been breached, violated, or deviated from" (Bruner 1991). The breach is what makes the events *reportable*.

**(iv) What it's like** (experientiality) — the narrative conveys what it is like to live through the storyworld-in-flux. It encodes "the pressure of events on real or imagined consciousnesses affected by the occurrences at issue" (p. 14). Without this dimension — without a consciousness experiencing the disruption — a temporal sequence of events is a chronicle or report, not a narrative.

## The gradience principle

These elements don't function as a checklist. They operate more-or-less: how detailed is the storyworld? How marked is the disruption? How vivid is the experiential dimension? A text that scores high on all four is a prototypical narrative. A text that scores low on "what it's like" (e.g., a synopsis) is less prototypically narrative — which is precisely what our pipeline works with.

## What this means for tvplotlines

Herman's model diagnoses our pipeline's blind spots:

- **Situatedness**: N/A — we analyze one medium (TV synopses) in one context
- **Event sequencing**: ✅ — this is what pass2 and pass4 do
- **Worldmaking/world disruption**: ⚠️ — we capture disruption implicitly through functions (inciting_incident, crisis) but don't track the *canonical expectation* that was breached
- **What it's like**: ❌ — our biggest gap. We capture *what happens* but not *what it's like to experience it*. Synopses strip this away, and our pipeline doesn't try to reconstruct it

The "canonicity and breach" concept is especially sharp: a narrative event is interesting because it *violates expectations within the storyworld*. This could refine our inciting_incident definition: not just "the event that starts the plotline" but "the event that breaches the canonical script of the storyworld."
