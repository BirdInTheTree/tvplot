# Actant model has six roles, not four

Source: Bal 1988, "Narratology", Ch. 3.3 Actors (pp. 166-172)

Greimas's actant model, as presented by Bal, replaces our Story DNA (hero, goal, obstacle, stakes) with six actants in three pairs:

## Subject ↔ Object

The teleological relation: who wants what. Subject = our "hero." Object = our "goal." But Object is broader — it can be a state ("becoming an independent woman"), knowledge ("the identity of the murderer"), or prevention ("preventing discovery").

| Subject | function | Object |
|---|---|---|
| John | wants to marry | Mary |
| Anna Wulf | wants to become | an independent woman |
| The old people | want to prevent | the discovery of their crime |
| Kinsey Millhone | wants to know | the identity of the murderer |
| The killer | wants to avoid | Millhone's discovery |
| Scheherazade | wants to prevent | the king's killing her |

## Power ↔ Receiver

Who enables or blocks the enterprise overall, and who benefits from the result. Power (Greimas's *destinateur*) is often abstract: society, fate, time, a character trait, social structure. Receiver (*destinataire*) is who benefits — not always the Subject.

Power vs Helper distinction:
| Power | Helper |
|---|---|
| Has power over the whole enterprise | Can give only incidental aid |
| Is often abstract | Is mostly concrete |
| Often remains in the background | Often comes to the fore |
| Usually only one | Usually multiple |

Examples: in a fairy tale, Power = "the king" who sets the quest; in a psychological novel, Power = "the subject's own character trait"; in a social novel, Power = "class structure."

## Helper ↔ Opponent

Who aids the Subject on the way to the Object, and who blocks. Opponent = our "obstacle," but more specific: Opponent blocks *on the way*, while Power enables/blocks *the whole enterprise*.

Helpers: each necessary but individually insufficient. Opponents: must be overcome one by one, but overcoming one doesn't guarantee success.

## What this means for tvplotlines

Story DNA has 4 fields; the actant model has 6. The additions are:
- **Power** — captures systemic/abstract forces (fate, institutions, character flaws) that our "obstacle" misses
- **Receiver** — captures who actually benefits, which can differ from the hero

For theme-led shows (The Wire, Succession), Power is especially useful: the real force driving the fabula isn't a character but "the institution" or "the system." Our current Story DNA struggles with these — "hero" and "obstacle" feel forced when the problem is systemic.
