# Competence determines when the subject can act

Source: Bal 1988, "Narratology", Ch. 3.3 Actors (p. 173)

Greimas subdivides the subject's competence — their ability to act — into three types:

- **Determination / will** — the subject *wants* to act
- **Power / possibility** — the subject *can* act
- **Knowledge / skill** — the subject *knows how* to act

The phase of the fabula where the virtual subject begins to *desire* the execution of the program is the **introduction** of the fabula. This reframes "inciting incident": it's not necessarily an external event that disrupts equilibrium. It's the moment the subject acquires *determination* — the will to pursue a program.

## Competence as differentiator

Different kinds of subjects are distinguished by which competence they primarily rely on. In fairy tales, the opposition between good and evil often maps to power/force vs knowledge/skill. In detective stories, the detective's competence is knowledge — and interestingly, the murderer's competence is *also* knowledge/skill, but the detective fails in their competence (makes a mistake) while the murderer succeeds in theirs.

## What this means for tvplotlines

We currently have "inciting_incident" as a single concept. But Bal/Greimas suggest it could be decomposed: when does the character gain *will* (determination), *ability* (power), and *knowledge* (skill)? In Breaking Bad, Walt gains determination (cancer diagnosis → will to provide) before he gains knowledge (cooking meth with Jesse) before he gains power (territory, connections). Each is a different structural moment.

This is probably too granular for the current pipeline but could inform character arc analysis.
