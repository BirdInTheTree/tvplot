# Suspense has four types, not one

Source: Bal 1988, "Narratology", Ch. 2.8 Focalization (pp. 148-149)

Bal analyzes suspense through the **asymmetry of knowledge** between reader/viewer and character. When a question is raised ("Who did it? How will it end?"), four situations are possible:

| reader | character | type | mechanism |
|---|---|---|---|
| doesn't know | doesn't know | **riddle** | detective story, search — both parties seek the answer |
| knows | doesn't know | **threat** | the audience sees the bomb, the character doesn't — tension from anticipated collision |
| doesn't know | knows | **secret** | the character holds information the audience lacks — tension from anticipated revelation |
| knows | knows | no suspense | — |

## Comparison with current tvplotlines

Our interaction types include "dramatic_irony" — which maps only to **threat** (reader+, character−). But riddle and secret are equally powerful narrative effects:

- **Riddle** drives mystery arcs: who killed Laura Palmer? What is the island? The audience and characters are equally in the dark.
- **Secret** drives revelation arcs: the old people in Couperus's *Of Old People* know about the murder but the reader doesn't. Information is gradually released through shifting focalization.
- **Threat** (= our dramatic_irony) drives tension arcs: we know Walt is Heisenberg, Hank doesn't.

## What this means for tvplotlines

Expanding interaction types from one (dramatic_irony) to three (riddle, threat, secret) would capture more narrative mechanics. Each type implies a different audience experience and could predict different structural patterns (mystery arcs tend to have delayed revelations; threat arcs tend to have convergence events).
