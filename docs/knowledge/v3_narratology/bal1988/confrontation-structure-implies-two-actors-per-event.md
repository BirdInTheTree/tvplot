# Confrontation structure implies two actors per event

Source: Bal 1988, "Narratology", Ch. 3.2 Events (pp. 157-158)

Every functional event has three components: two actors and one action. In logical terms: two arguments and one predicate. Linguistically: subject – predicate – (direct) object, where both subject and object are agents of action.

This is a strict requirement. "John kills a fly" — if the fly isn't an actor (doesn't cause or undergo functional events elsewhere in the fabula), this isn't a functional event despite having the right grammatical form. "Liz writes a letter" — a letter isn't an actor, but writing presupposes a reader, so the confrontation is implicit: "Liz writes to [someone]."

The confrontation criterion filters out isolated actions. John who "kills a fly" and never interacts with another actor functionally isn't participating in the fabula — he's "an isolated agent of action," illustrating a condition (loneliness) rather than driving the plot.

## What this means for tvplotlines

Our event definition doesn't require a second actor. "House orders a lumbar puncture" names only one actor. But by Bal's logic, it's functional because there's an implicit confrontation: House vs. the patient's illness, or House vs. Cameron's objection. Making the second actor explicit (even when it's implicit in the text) would sharpen event descriptions.
