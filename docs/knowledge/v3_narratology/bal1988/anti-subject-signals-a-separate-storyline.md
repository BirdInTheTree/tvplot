# Anti-subject signals a separate storyline

Source: Bal 1988, "Narratology", Ch. 3.3 Actors (pp. 172-173)

An anti-subject is not just an opponent. An opponent blocks the subject *incidentally* — their opposition is circumstantial. An anti-subject has **their own program**, their own aim, and this pursuit happens to cross-purposes with the subject's.

The structural difference: an opponent is defined by their relation to the subject's program. An anti-subject is defined by having **an autonomous program** that independently generates events.

## Example: Tom Thumb

The giant is not merely an opponent who blocks Tom's safe return. The giant has his own program: find children, eat them. He has his own competence (physical force, power of smell). His program intersects with Tom's because Tom *is* the children he wants to eat. Remove Tom from the story and the giant would still be hunting children.

## When lines cross

"The appearance of a separate subject indicates the existence of a sub-fabula." When an actant has their own aims and acts to achieve them, they're an autonomous subject — and their program is a separate fabula that intersects with the primary one. "There are moments in the fabula when the different lines touch or cross."

## What this means for tvplotlines

This is a precise criterion for plotline detection: **if a character has their own program (goal + actions toward it), they're an anti-subject, and their program is a separate plotline.** An opponent who merely blocks the hero doesn't get their own plotline — they're part of the hero's plotline.

In Game of Thrones: Cersei isn't just an opponent to Ned Stark. She has her own program (protect her children, maintain power). That program generates its own events. She's an anti-subject → separate plotline.

In House M.D.: the patient isn't an anti-subject. They don't have an autonomous program that generates events in the fabula. They're the Object of House's program. No separate plotline.

This test is sharper than our current heuristic (different heroes OR one hero with different goals). It asks: does this character *independently generate events*, or only react to the subject's program?
