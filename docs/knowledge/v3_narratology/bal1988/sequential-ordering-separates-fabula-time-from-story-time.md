# Sequential ordering separates fabula-time from story-time

Source: Bal 1988, "Narratology", Ch. 2.3 Sequential Ordering (pp. 67-84)

The order of events in the story can differ from their chronological order in the fabula. These differences — **anachronies** — are not mere stylistic choices but structural devices with specific narrative functions.

## Types of anachrony

**Direction:**
- **Retroversion** (flashback) — presenting a past event
- **Anticipation** (flashforward) — presenting a future event

**Distance:** how far from the story's "present" the anachrony reaches.

**Span:** how much fabula-time the anachrony covers (a moment vs. years).

**Relation to primary fabula:**
- **Internal** — within the time-span of the primary fabula
- **External** — outside it (backstory, aftermath)
- **Mixed** — starts outside, ends inside (or vice versa)

## Functions of retroversion

Internal retroversions serve specific structural purposes:
- **Filling a gap (paralipsis)** — supplying information that was skipped earlier
- **Repetition with reinterpretation** — the same event presented in a new light, changing its meaning
- **Providing antecedents** — backstory for a newly introduced character

External retroversions provide **antecedents** — the past of the actors, relevant insofar as it explains their current actions.

## What this means for tvplotlines

Our pipeline works with synopses that are mostly chronological. But TV episodes routinely use flashbacks and flash-forwards. If synopses note these, we could classify them using Bal's categories: is the flashback filling a gap, reinterpreting a past event, or providing antecedents? Each has different structural significance.

More practically: the distinction between internal and external anachrony maps to the difference between "events within the current season's timeline" and "backstory" — which our pipeline currently doesn't formally distinguish.
