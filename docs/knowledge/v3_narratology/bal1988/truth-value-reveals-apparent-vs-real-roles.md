# Truth value reveals apparent vs real roles of actants

Source: Bal 1988, "Narratology", Ch. 3.3 Actors (p. 174)

Actants have an **apparent** role and a **real** role, and these can diverge. A traitor appears to be a helper but is actually an opponent. A secret helper appears neutral but acts on the subject's behalf. A false hero appears to be the subject but lacks the competence.

Bal lists categories that emerge from this distinction:
- **Liars** — misrepresent their role
- **Master figures** — control from behind the scenes
- **False heroes** — appear to be subjects but aren't
- **Invisible fairies** — help without being perceived
- **Seducers** — manipulate through attraction
- **Truth-tellers** — reveal what others hide
- **False clues** — mislead about the fabula's direction
- **Sudden moments of inspiration** — unexpected knowledge that changes the subject's course

## What this means for tvplotlines

This is directly relevant to TV series with unreliable characters (GoT, Succession, Breaking Bad). Truth value *changes over the course of a season*: a character introduced as helper reveals themselves as opponent (or vice versa). These revelations are structurally significant events — they're not just "turning points" but specifically *truth-value shifts*.

Our pipeline could track this: does a character's actantial role change between episodes? If someone shifts from helper to opponent, that's a structural event that should be flagged.

Currently out of scope but worth noting for future work.
