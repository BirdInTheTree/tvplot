# Iterative anticipation describes procedural TV pilots

Source: Bal 1988, "Narratology", Ch. 2.3 Sequential Ordering (p. 84)

An **iterative anticipation** presents an event as the first in a series that will repeat: "Then the President appeared on the screen, a spectacle that would weigh on our spirits every Friday from now on."

The event is simultaneously unique (this specific appearance) and iterative (establishing a pattern). Bal notes a tension: "The more detailed such a report, the less credible its iterative character" — the more specific the case, the harder to believe it happens the same way every time.

## The procedural pilot

This is a precise description of what the pilot of a procedural show does. The first "case of the week" is an iterative anticipation: it's a specific, detailed case, but it also establishes the **formula** that will repeat. The audience understands after the pilot: *this is what happens every week.*

House S01E01 presents a specific patient with a specific mystery. But the audience leaves knowing: each week, a patient will arrive, House will resist, the team will run tests, diagnoses will fail, and House will have a last-minute insight. The pilot is iterative anticipation.

Bal's observation about the tension is relevant: the more vivid and specific the pilot's case, the more the audience engages with *this* case, and the less they register the iterative formula. Good procedural pilots balance both.

## What this means for tvplotlines

Our pass0 concept of "story_engine" is the formula behind the iterative anticipation. The story_engine describes *what repeats*, abstracted from the specific content of any one episode. "An antisocial maverick doctor does whatever it takes to solve puzzling cases" = the iterative pattern that the pilot anticipates.

This doesn't change our pipeline, but gives it a narratological foundation: format detection (pass0) is recognizing whether the pilot contains an iterative anticipation.
