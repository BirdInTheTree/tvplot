# The Most Reportable Event is the point of the story

Source: Ouyang & McKeown 2015, "Modeling Reportable Events as Turning Points in Narrative" (pp. 1-2), drawing on Labov 1967/1997, Prince 1973, Polanyi 1976

The **Most Reportable Event (MRE)** is the nucleus of the story — the most unusual event with the greatest emotional impact on the narrator and audience. It's the answer to "So what?" and the shortest possible summary of a story.

Polanyi (1976): "one does not produce a narrative text for no reason at all." The MRE is that reason.

Prince (1973) defines a minimal story as three parts: a starting state, an ending state, and the event that transforms one into the other. For a story to be interesting, that change-of-state event should be the MRE.

Labov (1967/1997) defines a well-formed narrative as a series of actions leading to the MRE. The narrative builds toward it — it's where the story has been heading all along.

## MRE vs climax

The MRE maps to our "climax" function, but with an important difference: climax is defined positionally (peak of conflict), while MRE is defined by **reportability** — what makes the story worth telling. A climax can be structurally necessary but unremarkable. An MRE is by definition remarkable.

The MRE test: "If I could only say one thing about this story, what would it be?" That's the MRE.

## What this means for tvplotlines

Our pass4 assigns `plot_fn: climax` to the peak event of each plotline's season arc. But we don't ask *why* that event is the peak — we just look for the structural turning point. The MRE concept suggests an additional question: **is this event the most reportable one?** The one that makes the plotline worth watching?

For plotline ranking (A/B/C in pass3), MRE identification could help: the A-story is the one whose MRE is most reportable.
