# Narrative deep structure is independent of medium

Source: Chatman 1980, "What Novels Can Do That Films Can't (And Vice Versa)", Critical Inquiry 7(1), pp. 121-140

"Narrative itself is a deep structure quite independent of its medium" (p. 121). The same story — Cinderella, for instance — can be actualized as verbal tale, ballet, opera, film, comic strip, pantomime. The deep structure (the fabula, the "what happened") transfers; what changes is the expression.

This is the founding premise of transmedial narratology: there exists a level of narrative organization (story/fabula) that is medium-independent, and a level (discourse/text) that is medium-specific.

## Double time structure

All narratives combine two independent time orders (p. 122):
- **Story-time** (*histoire*) — the chronological sequence of events in the fabula
- **Discourse-time** — the order of presentation in the text/film

Story-time is fixed by the fabula's logic. Discourse-time is freely manipulable. This independence is "precisely and only possible because of the subsumed story-time" — you can only rearrange events because they have a logical order to deviate from.

This maps directly to Bal's fabula-time vs story-time distinction and Todorov's succession principle.

## What this means for tvplotlines

Our pipeline works at the boundary between medium-specific and medium-independent levels. Synopses are **already translated** from TV (a visual/audio medium) into text (verbal medium). This translation has extracted the story-level information and mostly lost the discourse-level information (cinematography, editing rhythm, music, acting). 

We work with what Chatman would call the **asserted** narrative — someone has told us what happened, selecting and naming properties. We don't work with the **presented** narrative — the actual visual/audio experience. This means our pipeline is inherently working on the fabula level, with some story-level residue (the order in which synopses describe events often follows discourse-time, not story-time).
