# Films present while novels assert — and synopses re-assert

Source: Chatman 1980, "What Novels Can Do That Films Can't (And Vice Versa)" (pp. 126-128, 139)

Chatman draws a fundamental distinction between two modes of conveying narrative information:

**Assertion** (novels): The narrator *states* that something is the case. "The cart was tiny; it came onto the bridge." This is an explicit claim about properties and relations. The narrator selects which details to mention and in what order.

**Presentation/depiction** (films): The camera *shows* a state of affairs. "A film doesn't say, 'This is the state of affairs,' it merely shows you that state of affairs." Details aren't selected — they're simply present in the frame. The number of visual details is "potentially large, even vast" but viewers, preoccupied with the plot, register only those "salient to the plot as it unrolls in our minds" (p. 126).

Key consequences:
- In novels, the narrator controls which properties are *asserted* — you know exactly what's claimed
- In films, the camera *presents* everything in the frame — what viewers register is partly their own selection
- Films can't easily assert abstract properties ("she was pretty"); they can only show a face that viewers may or may not find pretty (p. 131)
- Novel narrators can assert evaluative qualities directly; filmmakers must find visual equivalents or use voice-over (which imports novelistic assertion into film)

## "Interest" point of view

Chatman introduces a POV concept beyond perceptual (where the camera is) and cognitive (what the character knows): **interest POV** — sharing a character's emotional concerns, their stakes, their desires (p. 134). "We become identified with the fate of a character, and even if we don't see things or even think about them from his or her literal perspective, it still makes sense to say that we share the character's point of view."

## What this means for tvplotlines

Our pipeline works with **re-asserted** narrative. The original TV show *presents* (Chatman's sense). The synopsis writer *asserts* (selects, names, orders). Our LLM then *re-asserts* (extracts, labels, structures).

Each step loses presentational information and adds assertive interpretation:
- **TV → synopsis**: loses visual/audio presentation, gains explicit assertion of what happened
- **Synopsis → pipeline output**: loses the synopsis writer's emphasis/selection, gains structural labels (plotline, function)

This means our pipeline necessarily works on an *already interpreted* version of the narrative. We're not analyzing the show — we're analyzing someone's assertion about the show. This is both a limitation (we lose experientiality, visual nuance) and an advantage (assertion is easier to parse than presentation).

The "interest POV" concept is relevant to our plotline assignment: an event belongs to the plotline whose character's **interests** are most at stake, not necessarily the character who physically acts.
