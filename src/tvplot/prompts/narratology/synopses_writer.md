# TASK

Write a 200–400 word synopsis of one TV episode.

# RULES

1. **Every sentence is a transition.** A transition is a change of state — someone or something is one way at the start, another way at the end. The verb is incidental; the change is the point. Bad: "Walt cooks meth." Good: "Walt cooks his first batch — Jesse, who tried solo before, now sees how much he didn't know."

2. **No interpretation, no inner monologue.** Report what happened, not what it meant. Bad: "The cancer doesn't change Walt's life; the secret does." Good: "Walt receives the diagnosis. He decides not to tell Skyler." Let the pipeline find the meaning.

3. **No literary metaphors.** Bad: "the teacher becomes the thief", "the recognition flips Walt's resolve". Good: "Walt steals chemistry equipment from school", "Walt sees the missing plate shard and changes his mind".

4. **Factual accuracy.** Use only what is in the source material. Don't invent scenes, don't speculate about motivations not stated. If the source is sparse — leave the detail out, don't fabricate.

5. **Causality is explicit.** Use "because", "so that", "after", "leading to". Don't make the reader guess.

6. **At least three plotlines per episode.** A-story plus at least two more. Personal arcs, institutional dynamics, runners. Even if a plotline gets one sentence, mention it. A 100% case-of-the-week synopsis is useless to the pipeline.

7. **Institutional matters.** If the show involves an organization (DEA, hospital, agency), capture what happens at that level — power moves, political pressure, internal conflicts. Don't treat it as background.

8. **Screen order.** Episodes interleave plotlines on screen. Preserve that order. Don't sort events by plotline.

# WHAT NOT TO INCLUDE

- Visual style, music, directing, editing
- Symbolism, themes, editorial commentary
- The word "Meanwhile"
- Future-episode spoilers
- Recap of past episodes (unless a character learns about them now)
- Author intuition about character psychology not shown on screen
- Drive/texture labels — that classification happens later in the pipeline

# OUTPUT

Plain text. 200–400 words. One paragraph per scene-cluster, blank lines between. Total transitions follow the natural density of the episode — no fixed target.

# SELF-CHECK

Before finalizing, re-read:

1. Does any sentence describe a state, atmosphere, or background? Cut it.
2. Does any sentence interpret what an event meant? Cut the interpretation, keep the event.
3. Does any sentence use a literary metaphor? Replace with the literal action.
4. How many distinct plotlines are present? If fewer than 3, something is missing.
5. For procedural/hybrid: is there serialized content (character arcs, institutional politics)? It must be at least 30% of the text.
6. Does every transition follow from the previous one or set up the next?

# EXAMPLE — Breaking Bad S01E03

Walt and Jesse clean up Emilio's body. Krazy-8 stays locked in the basement. Krazy-8 reveals that Jesse told him personal details about Walt; Walt confronts Jesse, who is smoking meth, and Jesse leaves angry.

Skyler tells Marie she is writing a story about a stoner and asks about marijuana. Marie assumes Walt Jr. is using drugs and tells Hank. Hank takes Walt Jr. to a motel and shows him a meth addict's teeth.

Walt phones Skyler from Jesse's house, claiming he is at the car wash. Skyler tells him she knows he quit two weeks ago and forbids him from coming home. Walt brings Krazy-8 food. He suffers a coughing fit and collapses. When he comes to, he tells Krazy-8 about the cancer. Krazy-8 talks about his father's furniture store. Walt decides to release him.

Walt notices a shard missing from the broken plate. He realizes Krazy-8 has hidden it as a weapon. Walt strangles Krazy-8 with the bike lock. Krazy-8 stabs Walt's leg during the struggle.

Walt drives home. Skyler is crying on the bed. She says he has something to tell her.

At the DEA, Hank and his team find the desert cook site and Krazy-8's car with meth inside. A Native American family hands Hank the gas mask their child found.
